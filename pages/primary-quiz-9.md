## 1.
<!--- 2016 (5) --->
::: problem id=1_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/1-square.png){image align="right"} 
Kathi draws a square with side length 10 cm.  

Then she joins the midpoints of each side to form a smaller square.  

What is the area of the smaller square? 

* [ ] 10 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 20 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 25 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 40 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [x] 50 cm<sup>2</sup>
{.col-5}

---
![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/1-square-answer.png){image align="right"} 
The area of the larger square is 10<sup>2</sup> = 100 cm<sup>2</sup>.  

By drawing on its diagonals, we can split the smaller square into 4 identical isosceles triangles.  

Observe that the larger square consists of 8 of these triangles.  

Therefore the smaller square has an area of `1/2 × 100 = 50` cm<sup>2</sup>.  
:::


## 2.
<!--- 2017 (7) --->
::: problem id=2_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/2-grid.png){image align="right"} 
Max colours in the squares of the grid, so that one third of all squares are blue and one half of all squares are yellow.  

The rest he colours in red.  

How many squares does he have to colour in red?  

* [ ] 1
* [ ] 2
* [x] 3
* [ ] 4
* [ ] 5
{.col-5}

---

In total there are 3 × 6 = 18 squares. 18 ÷ 3 = 6 are coloured blue and 18 ÷ 2 = 9 are coloured yellow.  

This leaves 18 – 6 – 9 = 3 to be coloured red.  
:::


## 3.
<!--- 2012 (11) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=1
Grandmother gave Vivian and Mike some apples and pears.  

In total they had 25 pieces of fruit.  

On the way home Vivian ate 1 apple and 3 pears, Mike ate 3 apples and 2 pears.  

At home they noted that there were exactly the same number of apples and pears in the basket.  

How many pears had grandmother given them?  

* [ ] 12
* [x] 13
* [ ] 16
* [ ] 20
* [ ] 21
{.col-5}

---

Subtracting those eaten on the way, at home they have 25 – 1 – 3 – 3 – 2 = 16 pieces of fruit.  

This means that at home they have 8 apples and 8 pears.  

Adding the pears already eaten shows that grandmother gave them 8 + 3 + 2 = 13 pears.  
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2017 (17) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/4-cubes.png){image align="right"} 
A big cube is made up of 9 identical building blocks.  

Each building block looks like the image to the right.   

Which big cube is possible? 

![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/4-cubes-options.png){image align="center"} 

* [x] A
* [ ] B
* [ ] C
* [ ] D
* [ ] E
{.col-5}

---

The big cubes B, C, D and E all contain sequences of colours (light or dark) which are impossible without moving around other blocks, for example:  

* In B, it is impossible for the column in the middle layer of the right-hand face to be all light.  
* In C, it is impossible for the central column on the top side and the side facing the reader to both be all dark.  
* In D, it is impossible for there to be one single dark cube on the top left of the side facing the reader.  
* In E, it is impossible for the back two cubes on the middle layer of the right-hand face to both be light.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2018 (24) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=5
![](/resources/primary-quiz-9/5-pattern.png){image align="right"} 
The figure shown on the right consists of one square part and eight rectangular parts.  

Each part is 8 cm wide.  

Peter assembles all parts to form one long, 8 cm wide rectangle.  

How long is this rectangle?  

* [ ] 150 cm
* [ ] 168 cm
* [ ] 196 cm
* [x] 200 cm
* [ ] 232 cm
{.col-5}

---

A new width of 8 cm means that all parts (the square, 4 smaller rectangles and 4 larger rectangles) must be assembled with their shorter 8 cm sides touching.  

The square has all lengths 8 cm.  

Each smaller rectangle has length 2 × 8 = 16 cm.  

Each larger rectangle has length 4 × 8 = 32 cm.  

The new length is then 8 + 4 × 16 + 4 × 32 = 200 cm.  
:::
