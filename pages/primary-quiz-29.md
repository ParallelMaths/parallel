## 1.
<!--- 2016 (4) --->
::: problem id=1_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-29/1-magnets.png){image align="right"} 
Lisa has mounted 7 postcards on her fridge door using 8 strong magnets (black dots).  

What is the maximum amount of magnets she can remove without any postcards falling on the floor? 

* [ ] 2
* [ ] 3
* [x] 4
* [ ] 5
* [ ] 6
{.col-5}

---

We can remove magnets so that each card is still held in place by at least one magnet.  

This means that we can remove the magnets on the:  
* Top right corner of the highest up card to pictures a church.  
* Top right and top left corners of the highest up card to picture a desert.  
* Bottom left corner of the lowest down card to picture a church OR the bottom left corner of the lowest down card to picture a desert (but not both).  

This totals a maximum of 4 magnets that can be removed.  
:::


## 2.
<!--- 2017 (6) --->
::: problem id=2_1 marks=1
Jane, Kate and Lynn go for a walk. Jane walks at the very front, Kate in the middle and Lynn at the very back.  

Jane weighs 500 kg more than Kate and Kate weighs 1000 kg less than Lynn.  

Which of the following pictures shows Jane, Kate and Lynn in the right order?  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-29/2-rhinos.png){image align="center"} 

* [x] A
* [ ] B
* [ ] C
* [ ] D
* [ ] E
{.col-5}

---

The only possibility is that Lynn is the heaviest, with Jane in the middle at 500 kg less and Kate being the lightest at 500 kg less than that.  

We are then looking for an image with the middle, smallest, largest (Jane, Kate, Lynn) which is option A.  
:::


## 3.
<!--- 2015 (11) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=2
Thomas has made the following shape with 6 squares of side length 1.

![](/resources/primary-quiz-29/3-shape.png){image align="center"} 

What is the perimeter of the shape? 

* [ ] 9
* [ ] 10
* [ ] 11
* [x] 12
* [ ] 13
{.col-5}

---

There are 3 full side lengths on both the left and right of the shape, as well as 1 full side length on the top and 3 full side lengths on the bottom.  

Observe that when the squares do not align perfectly, the shorter lengths left on either side of the upper squares still sum to a full side length.  

This occurs 2 times, once on the middle layer and one on the top layer.  

In total we have 3 × 2 + 1 + 3 + 2 = 12 side lengths each of size 1, making the perimeter 12.
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2017 (16) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
![](/resources/primary-quiz-29/4-squares.png){image align="right"} 
Raphael has three squares.  

The first one has side length 2 cm, the second one has side length 4 cm and one corner is the centre of the first square.  

The third square has side length 6 cm and one corner is the centre of the second square.  

What is the total area of the figure shown? 

* [x] 51 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 32 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 27 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 16 cm<sup>2</sup>
* [ ] 6 cm<sup>2</sup>
{.col-5}

---

The total area is given by the sum of the areas of the three squares subtract the area of the two square overlaps.  

Raphael’s three squares have areas of `2^2 = 2` <sup>2</sup>, `4^2 = 16` <sup>2</sup> and `6^2 = 36` <sup>2</sup>, respectively.  

Since the centre of the first square is the corner of the second, the smaller overlapping square has side length half that of the first square. This means that it has side length 1 cm and therefore an area of 1 <sup>2</sup>.  

Similarly, the larger overlapping square has side length 4 ÷ 2 = 2 cm and hence an area of 4 <sup>2</sup>.  

The total area is then 36 + 16 + 4 – 4 – 1 = 51 <sup>2</sup>.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2018 (23) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=3
Nick wants to split the numbers 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 into some groups so that the sum of the numbers in each group is equally big.  

What is the biggest number of groups he can build this way? 

* [ ] 2
* [x] 3
* [ ] 4
* [ ] 6
* [ ] Another number
{.col-5}

---

There are 9 numbers present that altogether sum to 2 + 3 + … + 10 = 54.  

The biggest number of groups will be formed when the sum of each group is as small as possible.  

The sum of each group must be a factor of the total sum of 54.  

The factors of 54 are 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18, 54.  

However, it must be possible to make this sum using the numbers present. Since 10 is present in the original list, the smallest possible sum for each group is 18.  

Indeed, one of the six possibilities for the groups would be {2, 6, 10}, {3, 7, 8} and {4, 5, 9}.  

This makes 3 groups, since 3 x 18 = 54.  
:::
