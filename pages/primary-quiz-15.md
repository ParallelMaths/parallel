## 1.
<!--- 2013 (6) --->
::: problem id=1_1 marks=1
In the following sum the same digit is used in each square:  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-15/1-sum.png){image align="center"} 

Which digit must be used so that the sum is correct?  

* [ ] 6
* [x] 4
* [ ] 7
* [ ] 9
* [ ] 8
{.col-5}

---

Since the answer ends in the digit 6 and the squares all represent the same digit, we are looking for a number that produces a final digit of 6 when multiplied by itself.  

For example, 6 × 6 = 36, but this is far too large since 66 × 6 = 360 + 36 = 396.  

Remember that 4 × 4 = 16 and also notice that 180 = 90 × 2 = 45 × 2 × 2 = 45 × 4.  

Indeed, 44 × 4 = 160 + 16 = 176, so the squares each represent the digit 4.  
:::


## 2.
<!--- 2011 (9) --->
::: problem id=2_1 marks=1
A shape is made by fitting together the four pieces of card with no overlaps.  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-15/2-shape-question.png){image align="center"} 

Which of the following shapes is not possible? 

![](/resources/primary-quiz-15/2-shape-answer.png){image align="center"} 

* [ ] A
* [ ] B
* [ ] C
* [ ] D
* [x] E
{.col-5}

---

![](/resources/primary-quiz-15/2-shape-solutions.png){image align="center"} 

For A, we can place the second piece (rotated 90 degrees clockwise) on the bottom right, connected on its left to the third piece. Next, we place the first piece (rotated 90 degrees clockwise) in the top left of the new shape with the fourth piece attached to the right of it. All four pieces have their longest side parallel to the bottom of the page.  

For B, we can combine the first, third, fourth and second pieces in that order to form one long rectangle.  

For C, we can combine the fourth piece (rotated 90 degrees) at the bottom, connected to the first piece (rotated 180 degrees). Next, moving from the corner to the right, we can place the second piece (rotated 90 anticlockwise) followed by the third piece.  

For D, we just put all the piece together in order, but with their longest side perpendicular to the bottom of the page and also with the second piece rotated 180 degrees.  

Shape E requires 3 rounded ends, but we only have 2 available, since 2 of our rounded ends occur on the same piece (the third).  
:::


## 3.
<!--- 2013 (13) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=2
36 children voted for five students from their class.  

Each child was only allowed to vote once.  

The winner received 12 votes, and the student placed last just 4 votes.  

If each student received a different number of votes, how many votes did the second placed student receive? 

* [ ] 8
* [x] 8 or 9
* [ ] 9
* [ ] 9 or 10
* [ ] 10
{.col-5}

---

Subtracting the votes that we already know, the middle three students received 36 – 12 – 4 = 20 votes between the three of them.  

The numbers of votes for each of the middle three students must be less than 12 and more than 4, whilst making sure that each of the three middle students receives a different number.  

We could have 5 votes for fourth place, 6 for third and then 20 – 5 – 6 = 9 votes for second place.  

Or, we could have 5 votes for fourth, 7 for third and then 20 – 5 – 7 = 8 votes for second place.  

There are no other possibilities that have a different number of votes for each of the three middle students.  
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2011 (18) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
The 8 corners of the shape in the picture are to be labelled with the numbers 1, 2, 3 or 4, so that the numbers at the ends of each of the lines shown are different.  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-15/4-shape.png){image align="center"} 

How often does the number 4 appear on the shape?  

* [ ] 1
* [ ] 2
* [ ] 3
* [x] 4
* [ ] 5
{.col-5}

---

The number 4 must be placed at all points connected by lines to both 1, 2 and 3.  

Moving around the edge of the shape clockwise from 1, we must have the number 4 on the point in between 1 and 2, the point in between 2 and 3 and the point immediately to the left of 3.  

This then means that the next point around the perimeter can be any number except 4.  

The final point, immediately to the left of 1, also has to be 4.  

This makes a total of four points labelled with the number 4.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2013 (21) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=3
If you start with three numbers, the 'addition machine' produces three new ones by adding together each pair of two. 

For instance from the numbers {3, 4, 6} the addition machine makes {10, 9, 7}.  

If you use the addition machine again these numbers become {16, 17, 19}.  

We feed the three numbers {20, 1, 3} into the addition machine and let the machine calculate 2013 times.  

What is the biggest possible difference between two of the three resulting numbers? 

* [ ] 1
* [ ] 2
* [ ] 17
* [x] 19
* [ ] 2013
{.col-5}

---

After feeding in {20, 1, 3}, the machine produces {4, 23, 21}. Next, we get {44, 25, 27}.  

We can notice that all numbers get bigger each time and that the biggest difference between the numbers is still the same size as the biggest difference in the first set of numbers {20, 1, 3}.  

Therefore, even after 2013 calculations, the biggest possible difference between any two of the three numbers will still be the same as in the first set of numbers, {20, 1, 3}, and so this is 20 – 1 = 19.  
:::
