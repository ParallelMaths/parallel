## 1.
<!--- 2016 (3) --->
::: problem id=1_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-28/1-wire.png){image align="right"} 
A 10 cm long piece of wire is folded so that every part is equally long (see diagram).  

The wire is then cut through in the two positions marked.  

How long are the three pieces created in this way? 

* [x] 2 cm, 3 cm, 5 cm 
* [ ] 2 cm, 2 cm, 6 cm 
* [ ] 1 cm, 4 cm, 5 cm
* [ ] 1 cm, 3 cm, 6 cm
* [ ] 3 cm, 3 cm, 4 cm
{.col-5}

---

The 10 cm long piece of wire is folded into 10 line segments, each of length 1 cm.  

After cutting, the first piece consists of 3 line segments and so it is 3 cm long.  

The middle piece is made up of 5 folded line segments with a total length of 5 cm.  

The far right piece is 2 cm long.  

This means that, in order, the three pieces have lengths 2 cm, 3 cm and 5 cm.  
:::


## 2.
<!--- 2016 (10) --->
::: problem id=2_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-28/2-squares.png){image align="right"} 
Robert has two equally big squares made of paper.  

He glues them together.  

Which of the following shapes can he not make? 

![](/resources/primary-quiz-28/2-squares-options.png){image align="center"} 

* [x] A
* [ ] B
* [ ] C
* [ ] D
* [ ] E
{.col-5}

---

Shape B can be formed by rotating one square by 45 degrees and placing it on top of another square that has not been rotated, such that the centres of each square are on top of each other.  

Shape D can be formed by overlapping the top right and bottom left corners of the two squares.  

Shape E can be formed by placing the two squares in line with their edges touching.  

If we rotate one square by 45 degrees and place one of its vertices over the centre of an unrotated square, then we will obtain shape C.  

We cannot obtain shape A in this way. This is because the diagonal of a square is longer than its side length by a factor of √2.  
:::


## 3.
<!--- 2015 (14) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=2
On Field Street there are 9 houses in a row. At least one person lives in each house.  

Each pair of neighbouring houses have at most 6 inhabitants.  

What is the maximum number of people living on Field Street? 

* [ ] 23
* [ ] 25
* [ ] 27
* [x] 29
* [ ] 31
{.col-5}

---

To maximise the number of people living on the street, we should make the number of inhabitants as big as possible whilst ensuring that pairs of neighbouring houses all sum to 6 inhabitants.  

Note that 3 inhabitants per house only sums to 3 × 9 = 27.  

We can increase this sum by alternating between houses with as many occupants as possible and houses with as few as possible.  

In order, the number of inhabitants of each house can be 5 – 1 – 5 – 1 – 5 – 1 – 5 – 1 – 5.  

This totals 5 × 5 + 4 × 1 = 29 whilst ensuring that each neighbouring pair sums to 5 + 1 = 6.
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2016 (19) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
![](/resources/primary-quiz-28/4-squares.png){image align="right"} 
The perimeter of the rectangle ABCD is 30 cm.  

Three more rectangles are added so that their centres are in the corners A, B and D and their sides are parallel to the rectangle (see diagram).  

The sum of the perimeters of these three rectangles is 20 cm.  

What is the length of the border of the shape (thick black line)? 

* [ ] 50 cm
* [x] 45 cm
* [ ] 40 cm
* [ ] 35 cm
* [ ] This cannot be calculated.
{.col-5}

---

At each of the points A, B and D, the centre of the smaller rectangle is the corner of the larger rectangle ABCD.  

This means that one quarter of the perimeter of the smaller rectangle is inside rectangle ABCD and three quarters of the perimeter is outside rectangle ABCD.  

This effect of this is that, to obtain the length of the new border, at each of the points A, B and D we have to add `3/4 - 1/4 = 1/2` of the perimeter of each smaller rectangle to the perimeter of rectangle ABCD.  

We know that the sum of the area of all three smaller rectangles is 20 cm.  

Therefore, the length of the border is given by `30 + 1/2 × 20 = 40` cm.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2018 (22) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=3
The sum of Kathi’s age and the age of her mother is 36.  

The sum of the age of her mother and the age of her grandmother is 81.  

How old was Kathi’s grandmother when Kathi was born? 

* [ ] 28
* [ ] 38
* [x] 45
* [ ] 53
* [ ] 56
{.col-5}

---

Adding Kathi’s age and that of her mother gives 36.  

Doing the same with her mother’s age and her grandmother’s gives 81.  

Taking the difference between these, Kathi’s mother’s age will cancel out, showing that the difference in age between Kathi and her grandmother is 81 – 36 = 45.  

Algebraically, if Kathi’s age is k, her mother’s m and her grandmother’s g, then k + m = 36 and m + g = 81.  

Subtracting these gives us that (m + g) – (k + m) = (g – k) + (m – m) = g – k + 0 = g – k = 81 – 36 = 45.  

Therefore, her grandmother was 45 when Kathi was born.  
:::
