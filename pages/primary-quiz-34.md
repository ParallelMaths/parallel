## 1.
<!--- 2017 (5) --->
::: problem id=1_1 marks=1
![](/resources/primary-quiz-34/1-isalnds.jpg){image align="right"} 
The 10 islands are connected by 12 bridges (see diagram).  

All bridges are open for traffic.  

What is the minimum number of bridges that need to be closed off, so that the traffic between A and B comes to a halt? 

* [ ] 1
* [x] 2
* [ ] 3
* [ ] 4
* [ ] 5
{.col-5}

---

![](/resources/primary-quiz-34/1-isalnds-answer.jpg){image align="right"} 
We can close the bridge on the lower part of island A.  

This prevents any traffic between A and B that travels through the lower section of islands.  

In addition, we close the bridge on the upper part of B.  

This prevents any traffic between A and B in the upper section of islands.
:::

How many times do you have to roll an ordinary die in order to be certain that at least one number is rolled twice?  

* [ ] 5
* [ ] 6
* [x] 7
* [ ] 12
* [ ] 18
{.col-5}

---

After 6 rolls, it is still possible to have only rolled each of the six possible numbers once.  

After one more roll, we will have definitely rolled at least one of these numbers twice, since we only have six options.
:::



## 3.
<!--- 2017 (14) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=1
Paul goes on a 5-day hiking trek.  

He starts on Monday and finishes on Friday.  

Every day he covers 2 km more than the day before.  

In total he hikes 70 km.   

Which distance does he cover on Thursday? 

* [ ] 12 km
* [ ] 13 km
* [ ] 14 km
* [ ] 15 km
* [x] 16 km
{.col-5}

---

In a 5-day trek, Wednesday is the middle day.  

On Thursday Paul walked 2 km more than on Wednesday, and on Tuesday 2 km fewer.  

Similarly, Paul will have walked 4 km more on Friday than on Wednesday, and 4 km fewer on Monday.  

We can see that there is symmetry in how far he walks across the 5 days, with the line of symmetry centred on Wednesday.  

This means that on Wednesday, Paul walked exactly his average distance per day, which is 70 ÷ 5 = 14 km.  

Hence, on Thursday he walked 14 + 2 = 16 km.  
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2018 (18) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
![](/resources/primary-quiz-34/4-sum.png){image align="right"} 
Instead of digits Hannes uses the letters A, B, C and D in a calculation.  

Different letters stand for different digits.  

Which digit does the letter B stand for?  

* [x] 0
* [ ] 2
* [ ] 4
* [ ] 5
* [ ] 6
{.col-5}

---

The answer has the digit D in the thousands’ column, yet the numbers that we are adding together have no digits in this column.  

Therefore, A + C must produce a carry to the thousands’ column.  

However, since we are only adding together two single digits, this carry can be at most 1.  

Therefore, D = 1.  

This means that A + C = 11.  

Looking at the tens’ column, we know that this column has received a carry of 1 from A + C in the units’ column, but that there is no carry to the hundreds’ column.  

The only possibility is that B = 0, so that B + B + 1 = D = 1.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2019 (24) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=3
Linus builds a 4 × 4 × 4 cube made up of 32 white and 32 black 1 × 1 × 1 cubes.  

He arranges the cubes so that the surface of the big cube has as much white as possible.  

Which fraction of the surface is white? 

* [x] `3/4`
* [ ] `2/3`
* [ ] `1/2`
* [ ] `3/8`
* [ ] `1/4`
{.col-5}

---

There are 4 × 4 × 4 = 64 smaller cubes making up the larger cube.

The centre of this larger cube is a 2 × 2 × 2 cube made up of eight 1 × 1 × 1 cubes that are not visible from the outside. These eight smaller cubes should all be black.

The remaining 64 – 8 = 56 smaller cubes make up the surface area of the larger cube, which we want to make as white as possible.
Note that the 1 × 1 × 1 cubes that form the corners of the larger cube each have 3 faces visible. Those smaller cubes forming the rest of the edges of the larger cube each have 2 faces visible. The remaining cubes, in the centre of each face, only have 1 face visible.

To make the white area as big as possible, we should put white 1 × 1 × 1 cubes in the 8 corners of the larger cube, leaving 32 – 8 = 24 white cubes to assign.

We then put white cubes along the edge of the larger cube. This means putting 2 white cubes in the middle of each of the 12 edges of the larger cube. This totals 2 × 12 = 24 white cubes.

Note that the 2 × 2 squares in the centres of each of the 6 faces of the larger cube are all formed by black 1 × 1 × 1 cubes, which uses up the remaining 24 black cubes.

Now, the eight corner cubes have 3 white faces showing. The 24 edge cubes have 2 white faces showing. This totals 3 × 8 + 2 × 24 = 72 white 1 × 1 faces on the surface of the larger cube.

The total surface area of all 6 faces is 6 × 4 × 4 = 96.

The fraction of the surface that is white is therefore 72 ÷ 96 = 3/4.

:::
