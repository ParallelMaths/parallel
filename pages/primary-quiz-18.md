## 2.
<!--- 2015 (9) --->
::: problem id=2_1 marks=1
Each plant in Johns garden has exactly 5 leaves or exactly 2 leaves and a flower.  

In total the plants have 6 flowers and 32 leaves.  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-18/2-flowers.png){image align="center"} 

How many plants are growing in the garden? 

* [x] 10
* [ ] 12
* [ ] 13
* [ ] 15
* [ ] 16
{.col-5}

---

There are 6 flowers in total, meaning that 6 of the plants are ones which have a flower and 2 leaves.  

Therefore, these plants will have 6 × 2 = 12 leaves between them.  

This means that 32 – 12 = 20 leaves come from the plants without flowers.  

These plants have exactly 5 leaves and so there are 20 ÷ 5 = 4 plants without flowers.  

Overall, we have 6 + 4 = 10 plants.  

:::


## 3.
<!--- 2013 (15) --->
::: problem id=3_1 marks=2
How many subtraction expressions of two-digit numbers have a difference of 50?

* [x] 40
* [ ] 30
* [ ] 50
* [ ] 60
* [ ] 10
{.col-5}

---

The subtraction involving the smallest two-digit numbers is 60 – 10 = 50.  

The subtraction involving the largest two-digit numbers is 99 – 49 = 50.  

The number of different sums possible is then the same as counting the number in the list 10, 11, 12, ... , 49.  

This gives 40.  
:::


## 4.
<!--- 2013 (19) --->
::: problem id=4_1 marks=2
Numbers are written in the 4×4 grid: any two numbers in neighbouring squares should have a difference of 1, that is squares that share an edge. 

The number 3 is already given. 

The number 9 will be used somewhere in the grid.  

![](/resources/primary-quiz-18/4-grid.png){image align="center"} 

How many different numbers will have been used once the grid is filled in completely? 

* [ ] 4
* [ ] 5
* [ ] 6
* [x] 7
* [ ] 8
{.col-5}

---

![](/resources/primary-quiz-18/4-grid-answer.png){image align="right"} 
The square already labelled 3 is in the top left of the grid.  

The bottom right square is 6 moves away from the top left (3 rights and 3 downs, in some order). Each time we move to the next square, the value increases by 1.  

9 is 6 more than 3 and the only square 6 moves away from the top left is the bottom right.  

This means that 9 can only be in the bottom right square and therefore we will use all the numbers from 3 to 9 in the grid, including 3 and 9.  

We have used the numbers from 3 to 9 inclusive, which totals 7 different numbers.  
:::


## 5.
<!--- 2016 (22) --->
::: problem id=5_1 marks=3
![](/resources/primary-quiz-18/5-circles.png){image align="center"} 
Kirsten has written numbers into 5 of the 10 circles.  

She wants to write numbers into the remaining circles so that the sum of the three numbers along every side of the pentagon is always the same.  

Which number does she have to write into the circle marked `X`?  

* [ ] 7
* [ ] 8
* [ ] 11
* [x] 13
* [ ] 15
{.col-5}

---

The trick is to examine the corner circles where the sides meet.  

If two sides share a circle at a corner, then the sum of the other two circles in a side must be the same as the sum of the other two circles in the adjacent side.  

In the top left side, 3 and 7 sum to 10. But the top left side shares a circle with the bottom left side (the middle left corner), meaning that the other empty circle (the bottom left corner) must be 10 – 1 = 9.  

We now consider the bottom side (now containing 9 and 6) and the bottom right side (containing X and 2).  

Since these two sides share the circle in the bottom right corner, their other circles must sum to the same amount.

Therefore, X + 2 = 9 + 6 = 15, giving X = 13.  
:::
